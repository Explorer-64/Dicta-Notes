# Bot Detection Guide for Community Engagement
### For Jill — Dicta-Notes Community Management

The goal: don't waste time replying to bots. Real engagement builds community; replying to bots does nothing.

---

## Step 1 — Check the Bio First (30 seconds)

Before reading a single word of the post, click the author's profile.

**Bot signals in a bio:**
- Account created recently (days or weeks ago) but already has dozens of posts
- **5+ posts per day** — no human writes 5 polished listicles daily. If you see this, stop and move on.
- Bio is vague and generic: "Developer | Productivity enthusiast | I write about tools"
- No real photo, or a suspiciously perfect stock-looking headshot
- No links to real projects, GitHub, LinkedIn, or a personal site
- Follower/following ratio is off — following thousands, very few followers back
- No comments or replies on their own posts — bots post and disappear

**Real person signals in a bio:**
- Specific job title, company, or project mentioned
- Personal website or GitHub with actual activity
- Posts about varied topics including opinions, questions, struggles
- Engages in comments on their own and other people's posts

---

## Step 2 — Check the Post Pattern (1 minute)

Scroll their post history. Ask:

| Question | Bot Answer | Human Answer |
|---|---|---|
| How often do they post? | 3–10x per day | A few times a week at most |
| What format are the posts? | Always listicles ("7 apps for X") | Mix of opinions, questions, tutorials, discussions |
| Do they reply to comments? | Rarely or never | Yes, with specific responses |
| Are posts all the same length? | Yes, suspiciously uniform | Varies naturally |
| Do they ever ask questions? | No — only broadcasts | Yes |
| Is there a personal opinion anywhere? | No — just neutral summaries | Yes |

---

## Step 3 — Read the Post Itself (if still unsure)

**The legitimacy sandwich** — the most common bot technique:
> Mix 3–4 famous, well-known apps/tools as credibility anchors, then slide in 2–3 obscure paid products as the real promotional targets.

Look for:
- Specific pricing mentioned in editorial content ("$5 lifetime", "$15/mo") — this is paid placement
- Every entry is exactly the same paragraph length — humans vary
- Generic closing line fishing for engagement: "What's your setup? Drop it in the comments 👇"
- No actual personal experience described — just feature summaries anyone could copy from a landing page
- The post could have been written by someone who never used any of the products

---

## Step 4 — Decision

| Signal Count | Action |
|---|---|
| 3+ bot signals from bio alone | Skip — don't engage |
| 5+ posts/day from this account | Skip immediately — definite bot |
| Real bio but suspicious post | Check post pattern before engaging |
| All signals point to real person | Engage genuinely |

---

## What Good Engagement Looks Like

Worth replying to:
- Someone asking "does X work offline?" or "is there a free transcription tool?"
- A genuine review or frustration post about Otter.ai, Fireflies, or similar
- A developer asking about multilingual apps for their team
- Someone in r/freelance, r/journalism, r/medicine asking for tool recommendations

Not worth replying to:
- Any "7 apps for X" listicle from a new account
- Any post that includes Dicta-Notes alongside unrelated apps in a promotional list
- Any account posting more than 3x per day with no real replies

---

## Quick Reference — Red Flags at a Glance

🚩 5+ posts per day
🚩 Account less than 2 months old with 50+ posts
🚩 Bio is vague with no real links
🚩 Never replies to comments
🚩 Every post is a listicle
🚩 Pricing mentioned in editorial content
🚩 Famous apps used as credibility anchors alongside obscure ones
🚩 No personal opinion or experience anywhere in the post
🚩 Closing line is always generic engagement bait
